How the Electoral College Skews Voting Power

If you're American and over the age of 18, you have probably become very familiar with this map in the past year. It shows the results of the 2016 Presidential Election, with each state colored by its winner.
Though Trump recieved almost 3 million fewer votes across the country than his opponent, key wins in Michigan, Florida, and Pennsylvania alllowed him to achieve the 270 electoral votes necessary to win the election.
This makes Trump the 5th person to ever become the president of the US while losing the popular vote, joining the ranks of John Quincy Adams (1824), Rutherford B. Hayes (1876), Benjamin Harrison (1888), and George W. Bush (2000).
These discrepencies are possible due to the eccentric electoral college system used in the presidential election.
Most states use a Winner Takes All system. As the name suggests, the candidate who gets more votes in the general election receives all of the state's electoral votes, regardless of the winning margin.
Only two states use a Congressional District system. Maine and Nebraska award one vote to the winner of each of their congressional districts. The remaining two votes in each state are then assigned to the overall winner in the state.
In this map, the area of each state is proportional to the number of electoral the state has. This perspective, and other cartograms, or maps that rescale the area of states based on their number of votes, like it, makes it look like states with high populations have a disproportionate voting power.
One criticism of the Electoral College is that it concentrates electoral power in large states. The four most populated states--California (55), Texas (38), Florida (29), and New York (29)--hold a total of 151 electoral votes, or 28% of the 538 electoral votes, while eight regions (seven states and Washington DC) have just 3 votes each.
Because of this, campaigns typically focus on a small number of states that are moderate and have a significant number of electoral votes. In the 2016 election, for example, 90% of campaign events by Clinton and Trump were concentrated in just 11 states, according to FairVote.
Just four states made up 70% of all Clinton campaign events about half of Trump's.
An alternative analysis of the electoral college tells a different, though equally problematic, story.
By dividing the voting population of each state (from the 2015 census) by its number of electoral votes, each state's voting power per person can be estimated. For example, Wyoming has a rating of 3.09, meaning each voter in Wyoming has the power that three people would have in a simple one-person-one-vote system. California, on the other hand, has a rating of 0.84, meaning it takes 3.67 people in California to match the voting power of one person in Wyoming.
Thus, this map instead scales the area of each state by its voting power per person. States with lower populations have significantly higher power than states with higher populations. Wyoming is the most extreme example, but voters in Alaska, North Dakota, South Dakota, Vermont, Rhode Island, and Washington D.C. enjoy ratings over 2.
Admittedly, this analysis simplifies certain notable variables. It fails to account for the voting record for each state. In that respect, a voter in Wyoming, which has voted for the Republican candidate every year since 1964, could be considered to have a lower chance to be the deciding electoral vote, and thus a lower effective voting power, while a swing voter in a swing state holds a disproportional amount of power.
However, this visualization shows how the electoral college distorts the voting power of citizens based along state lines. It also, incidentally, helps illustrate how a candidate who loses the popular vote can still win by succeeding in states with fewer voters, but higher electoral power per voter.
In a country that holds the ideal that all Americans should be given an equal vote, it seems to make little sense to that the weight of each person's vote should be based on their geographic location, a factor that is more likely to be determined by birth than by choice.

In the presidential elections, minority voters in American already vote at a lower rate than White Americans, according to the United States Elections Project. The electoral college further reinforces that problem by reducing the voting power each minority voter is able to exert.

Just sore losers?

Ending the electoral college should be an issue that spans party lines.
Yes, Hillary Clinton has called for ending the electoral college in an interview with CNN...
But so, too, did Donald Trump in 2012.

The electoral college is a disaster for a democracy.

— Donald J. Trump (@realDonaldTrump) November 7, 2012
Yes, almost 5 million Democratic voters petitioned electors to defy their electoral college mandates, but so, too, did Republicans lobby electors in 2008 to vote against Barack Obama in spite of their mandates.
Instead, the call for obolishing the outdated electoral college is an issue motivated by restoring the one person, one vote ideal that holds all Americans equal in the eyes of the government.
Of course, all voting systems have their flaws, but rethinking the importance of the electoral college just might make America's a little more equitable.
