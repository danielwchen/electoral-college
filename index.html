How the Electoral College Skews Voting Power

If you're American and over the age of 18, you have probably become very familiar with this map. It shows the results of the 2016 Presidential Election, with each state colored by its winner. Though Donald Trump recieved almost 3 million fewer votes than Hillary Clinton, key wins in Michigan, Florida, and Pennsylvania alllowed him to achieve the 270 electoral votes necessary to win the election. This makes Trump the 5th person to ever become the president of the US while losing the popular vote.

This was possible due to the Electoral College.

The Electoral College, a system intentionally formed as a buffer between the popular vote and the presidential selection, imbalances voters' access to candidates and individual voting power.
Each state has a number of electors equal to its total number of Congresspeople.
One criticism of the Electoral College is that it concentrates electoral power in large states. The four most populated states--California (55), Texas (38), Florida (29), and New York (29)--hold a total of 151 electoral votes, or 28% of the 538 electoral votes, while eight regions (seven states and Washington DC) have just 3 votes each.
Only two states use a Congressional District system. Maine and Nebraska award one vote to the winner of each of their congressional districts. The remaining two votes in each state are then assigned to the overall winner in the state.
Most states assign their electors using a Winner Takes All system. As the name suggests, the candidate who gets more votes in the general election receives all of the state's electoral votes, regardless of the winning margin.
Because of this, campaigns typically focus on a small number of states that are moderate and have a significant number of electoral votes. In the 2016 election, for example, 90% of campaign events by Clinton and Trump were concentrated in just 11 states, according to FairVote.
Just four states made up 70% of all Clinton campaign events and about half of Trump's.
Not only does the Electoral College cause campaigns to be distributed unevenly, it also distributes voting power unequally.
Dividing a state's number of electoral votes by its population (from 2015 estimates from the US Census Bureau) allows each state's voting power per person to be approximated and compared to a one-person-one-vote system. Wyoming, for example, has a rating of 3.09, which means that each person voting in Wyoming has three times the voting power in the Electoral College than in a popular vote.
In this map the area of each state is scaled by its voting power per person. Wyoming has its area scaled to three times the original value. California's area, on the other hand, has an area that is 84% of its original.
Of course, this analysis simplifies certain notable variables. Voting in a swing state, for example, gives a voter much more impact than in a state that has a consistent voting history. Voting rates also differ between states. However, this map illustrates how the Electoral College unequally distributes voting power.
In a country that holds the ideal that all Americans should be given an equal vote, it seems to make little sense to that the weight of each person's vote should be based on their geographic location, a factor that is more likely to be determined by birth than by choice.

How the Electoral College Impacts Power by Demographics

Another negative side effect of the Electoral College is its suppression of minority voting power. The distribution of minorities across different states compounds the already difficult challenge of making minority voices heard.

By applying the voting power per voter for each state and race statistics from the US Census Bureau's data for 2015, it is possible to estimate the potential voting power per voter by race.

This gray person represents the voting power that one person would have if the presidential election were based on the popular vote.
The voting power of White Americans is increased by 3% by the Electoral College.
The voting power of Black Americans is about the same, with a slight increase of 0.4%.
The voting power of Hispanic Americans is decreased by 8%.
And the voting power of Asian Americans is decreased by 12%.
Remember, these statistics are calculated as potential electoral voting power. In other words, if every eligible voter cast a ballot in a presidential election, then, on average, it would take 17 extra Asian American votes to equal the votes of 100 White Americans. However, minority voters tend to vote at a lower rate. This can be due to a variety of economic or institutional reasons.
The figures added to the chart represent the voting rate by race. The baseline figure represents the average voting rate for all Americans.
Minority voters in American already vote at a lower rate than White Americans, according to the United States Elections Project. The Electoral College further exacerbates that problem by reducing the voting power each minority voter exerts.

Just sore losers?

Ending the Electoral College should be an issue that concerns people of all parties, not just Democrats.

Both Hillary Clinton and Donald Trump have criticized the Electoral College.

The electoral college is a disaster for a democracy.

— Donald J. Trump (@realDonaldTrump) November 7, 2012

Both parties have tried, and failed, to beseech the Electoral College to carry out its original purpose, a task that would be a Constitutional crisis. Almost 5 million Democratic voters petitioned electors to defy their Electoral College mandates in 2016, as did Republicans leverage electors in 2008 to vote against Barack Obama.

Instead, the call for obolishing the outdated Electoral College should be an issue motivated by restoring the one person, one vote ideal that holds all Americans equal in the eyes of the government.
